the zero waste kit

what to bring

the hardest part of zero waste shopping is remembering the right things. once it becomes a habit - bag by the door, jar in the backpack - the friction disappears.

at the farmers market
mesh produce bags
loose fruit and veg go straight in. if i'm stopping by uppercrrust, i can use my mesh bag for the bread/pastries too.
cloth bag or pillowcase for bread
any pillowcase works. most bakery vendors at the davis farmers market will put your loaf directly into it. uppercrrust is also a good place to get freshly baked bread and pastries without packaging, not all of their bread is but you can call in advance to have them set aside a loaf for you without packaging to pick up.
tote bag
obvious, but useless if it stays at home. keep one in your bag permanently. on each of my commonly used purses and bags i have a clip-on collapsible tote so i never forget it. if i'm biking i use my pannier duchy market bag instead as i can wear it like a sholder sling bag and then clip it on to my bike when it's time to head home.
at the co-op
a couple of these are shaped by how i eat, i'm vegetarian, so dairy and eggs show up below. if that's not your diet, the same habits still apply elsewhere: vegans can bring jars for oats, nut butters, tofu, and grains from the same bulk bins. meat eaters can bring a container straight to the butcher counter or deli and ask them to weigh it and fill it there, the same way you'd tare a jar for bulk goods. the method is the same no matter what's going in the container.
glass jars
weigh empty at the counter, fill from the bulk bins, pay by weight. wide-mouth mason jars work best for grains, spices, nuts, coffee. bring a few different sizes.
a reusable funnel
the co-op keeps paper funnels by the bulk bins, but they're primarily single-use. i use the base of an IKEA ÖVERST steel coffee filter ↗ - just the base, not the filter mesh part. since it's stainless steel and dishwasher safe i can use it over and over again for multiple purposes such as making my coffee but it also works great as a funnel on any jar or bottle. it's very useful when working with dry goods like oats, rice, and beans or placing spices in a smaller lid container without creating a mess.
empty bottles for the refill station
bring your own bottles for soap, shampoo, and cleaning products. you can use jars as well if you haven't bought a particular product before. weigh the empty container first at the tare station near the refill section, then fill. i am a die hard fan of dr bronner's soaps in both the baby unscented and peppermint scents so i reuse by plastic bottle i bought from dr bronners and refill them with their respective scents since i more easily recognize the scent based on label color. i make sure to cross out the barcode on all of my bottles before refilling so it doesn't get mistaken for a new packaged product, refilling is much cheaper than buying new.
your old dairy containers if applicable
at the davis food co-op they carry straus branded dairy in returnable glass. it comes with a (now increased) $3 deposit fee at checkout, which is not often factored into the price label at the store depends on which store you go to. you pay the bottle deposit at checkout and when you are finished return the empty bottle on your next visit. most places such as davis food co-op will have you get your deposit back through a refund at checkout, i place the milk bottle last and i get my $3 back on my recipt. make sure you check with your local store for their specific policies as some require an extra step of visiting customer service to get your deposit back before checkout. so far in my experience davis food co-op is the only place in davis with returnable glass containers for dairy.
empty egg carton
davis food co-op has eggs in bulk as well, i cross out the barcode as well on the carton before refilling so it doesn't get mistaken for a new packaged product. instead of the co-op i also opt for finding excess eggs from friends who have chickens!
how to refill at the bulk station
every store runs this a little differently. davis food co-op has a self-serve scale and lists weight in both lb and oz, with numbered bins. berkeley bowl tares for you at the counter, also in both lb and oz, prints a sticker with the weight, you fill it, then they weigh and re-sticker it at checkout. reup refills tares in oz only and skips bin numbers entirely, you just tell them the product name. the steps below are the general shape of it. ask a staff member what's different at your store, most are happy to walk you through it the first time.
step 1
weigh your empty container
before you fill anything, bring your empty jar or bottle to the scale near the bulk section (usually right by the entrance to bulk or the refill wall) and note the "tare" weight, that's the weight of the container itself, so you're not charged for it. some stores weigh it themselves rather than letting you use the scale.
step 2
label it with the tare weight
write the tare weight directly on a piece of masking tape on the jar, or on a scrap of paper from the pad at the scale. some stations print a small sticker with the weight and a bin/PLU number, stick that on instead.
step 3
fill from the bin or dispenser
for dry bulk bins, use the attached scoop to fill your jar, then close the bin lever fully so nothing spills for the next person. for the liquid refill station, place your bottle under the pump nozzle and dispense slowly - most pumps measure by the pull, not a steady stream.
step 4
note the item or bin number
jot down the bin number or product name so the cashier can ring it up correctly, most bulk bins have a number and a PLU code posted right on the label above the bin. some stores skip bin numbers entirely and just want the product name.
step 5
check out like normal
the cashier weighs your full container, subtracts the tare weight you noted, and charges you only for the product. keep the jar, next time you refill, it's already tared and ready to go.
everyday
travel mug
Mishka's, Pachamama Coffee, and Peet's will all fill your own mug in Davis - Starbucks will too, with a tracking sticker on the cup. worth checking the places tab for which ones also serve "for here" drinks in real glassware, since that policy varies by shop (and sometimes by hot vs. cold drink).
silicone bags or containers
replace ziplock bags for leftovers, snacks, lunches. Stasher bags are the most popular - dishwasher safe, last years. see the products tab.
safety razor
a one-time purchase that eliminates plastic cartridges permanently. blades are recyclable - take them to Recology (not the curbside bin) when you have a pile.
worth buying

starter swaps

this isn't a shopping list. most zero waste is about buying less. these are the specific things where a one-time purchase genuinely eliminates years of disposables.

kitchen
Stasher Bags
silicone zip bags that replace ziplock for leftovers, snacks, meal prep. dishwasher and microwave safe, last for years. available at the co-op or at stasherbag.com.
Beeswax Wraps
replaces plastic wrap for covering bowls and wrapping cheese. reusable for a year or more, compostable at end of life. Marleys Monsters makes good ones - see below.
Cloth Produce Bags
for bulk bins and the farmers market. mesh or muslin, washable. a set of 5–6 handles almost everything.
cleaning & body care
Dr. Bronner's Castile Soap
one product that replaces dozens - body wash, shampoo, dish soap, household cleaner, laundry. buy the large jug, refill smaller bottles from it. available in bulk at the co-op, so you can bring a jar. drbronner.com.
Shampoo & Conditioner Bars
lather like regular shampoo, no plastic bottle. last longer than liquid bottles per wash. available at the co-op and various online shops.
Safety Razor
the handle is a one-time purchase (Merkur, Edwin Jagger, or Rockwell are reliable starting points, ~$30–45). only the double-edge blades replace. blades are recyclable at Recology - never in the curbside bin.
Sunscreen (Attitude / Badger Balm)
mineral sunscreen, mostly plastic tubes with a few metal-tin options depending on the line. I use and love both, Attitude for daily wear and Badger Balm for anything more active. attitudeliving.com, badgerbalm.com.
sunscreen is the one place I'd tell you to set all of the above aside if it doesn't work for you. there's real discourse right now about mineral sunscreen leaving a white cast or a heavy texture, and I feel that firsthand as someone with brown skin, the cast shows up more on me and I've just made peace with dealing with it. but sun protection matters more than packaging. buy whatever sunscreen you'll actually wear and reapply, even if that means a plastic bottle from the drugstore. if you want to push on the packaging side, email the company directly and ask for a metal tin or refill option, that does more good than skipping sunscreen to avoid plastic.
paper replacements
Marleys Monsters UNpaper Towels
reusable cloth "paper" towels that snap together on a roll to look and work exactly like regular paper towels. one of the best-known zero waste swaps. marleysmonsters.com.
Reusable Cotton Rounds
replaces single-use cotton pads for makeup removal or skincare. washable and reusable for years. Marleys Monsters makes a good set.
Bamboo Toothbrush
handle composts; pull the nylon bristles before composting. available at the co-op. not a huge category, but a permanent no-effort swap.
good online store
Marleys Monsters
small-batch zero waste goods made in Eugene, OR. UNpaper towels, cloth napkins, cotton rounds, beeswax wraps, produce bags, travel kits. a reliable first stop when you want the reusable version of something disposable. ships from the Pacific Northwest - marleysmonsters.com.
good online store
Zerra & Co.
zero waste makeup brand, online-only but with a real container return program, send back your empty glass or metal containers for store credit instead of tossing them. I order eyeliner here, and the mascara (glass vial, refillable) is worth a look too. zerraco.com.
make it yourself

diy

a few things are cheaper and just as good homemade, and they're a good excuse to finally use up the empty jars you've been saving. start small, this isn't meant to replace everything on the starter swaps tab.

body care
Baking Soda Deodorant
the fastest jar-to-recipe ratio on this whole site. mix it, apply it, done. a good use for an empty Booda Butter jar or any small glass jar you've got sitting around.
what you need
baking soda tea tree oil, a few drops reused glass jar reusable cotton round or clean fingers
method
1stir baking soda with a few drops of tea tree oil in your jar until it's a slightly damp, sandy texture. add more of either until you like the consistency.
2pat a small amount onto dry underarms with a reusable cotton round or clean fingers. a little goes a long way.
3keep the jar somewhere dry. since you're only using a pinch at a time, it lasts a long while.
baking soda can irritate sensitive skin. patch test on your inner arm first, and if it stings or causes a rash, skip this one, a store-bought sensitive-skin deodorant is a perfectly good call instead.

ethical clothing
1
buy secondhand first
Community Mercantile and Aggie Reuse Store before buying anything new. Buy Nothing Davis before spending any money at all. Depop and ThredUp for secondhand online, ask your reseller to package your item in eco-friendly or recyclable materials.
2
repair what you have
a torn seam or broken zipper isn't a reason to throw something away. yololab Repair Café can teach you how to do small clothing repairs. basic hand-stitching takes 20 minutes to learn and fixes 80% of clothing problems. you can even repair your own socks through a technique called "darning".
3
if buying new, choose natural fibers
synthetic fabrics (polyester, nylon, acrylic) shed microplastics every wash. organic cotton, linen, wool, and hemp don't. look for GOTS certification.
4
better brands when buying new
Patagonia (repair program, used gear), The North Face (repair program), Eileen Fisher (take-back program), Prana (fair trade + recycled), Thought Clothing (organic + natural fibers). none are perfect but all are substantially better than fast fashion.
!
avoid fast fashion
Shein, Zara, H&M, and similar brands rely on extremely short production cycles, low wages, and low-quality materials designed to be worn a few times and discarded. the cost is paid elsewhere, in labor and in waste.
